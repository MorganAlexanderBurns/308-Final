What is the Gospel?

The word gospel comes from the greek work: "euangelion" which means good news. The gospel is the Good news of Jesus Christ and how he died for sinners so that they could be restored back into right relationship with God.

Why the Gospel matters

The Gospel, or "good news," holds profound significance in Christianity, shaping the beliefs and practices of millions globally. It conveys a message of redemption, offering hope for reconciliation and eternal life through faith in Jesus Christ. Jesus' teachings provide a moral compass, influencing ethical behavior and shaping community values. The Gospel addresses life's meaning, instilling purpose and a spiritual perspective. Emphasizing community and fellowship, it fosters supportive bonds among believers. Additionally, the Gospel teaches the concept of spiritual transformation, guiding individuals toward a more virtuous and purposeful existence.

Who is Jesus

Jesus Christ, a central figure in Christianity, born around 4 to 6 B.C. in Bethlehem, Judea, is regarded as the faith's founder. His teachings at age 30 focused on love, forgiveness, and the Kingdom of God, often conveyed through parables. His ministry included miracles such as healing the sick. Despite opposition, Jesus was crucified around A.D. 30, a pivotal event symbolizing redemption. Christians believe in his Resurrection three days later, celebrating annually on Easter. Jesus' teachings have profoundly influenced history, shaping Christianity into a major world religion, and his imp act extends beyond religious boundaries, influencing cultures and ethics throughout the centuries.

What is the Bible

The Bible, a central text in Christianity, comprises the Old Testament and the New Testament. The Old Testament includes historical narratives, poetry, and prophecy, covering foundational stories such as creation and the exodus. The New Testament focuses on Jesus' life, teachings, death, and resurrection, including Gospels, Acts, and Epistles. Christians view the Bible as divinely inspired and authoritative, providing guidance and salvation. Interpretation varies, with some passages taken literally and others metaphorically. The Bible plays a central role in worship, moral guidance, and shaping a Christian worldview. The transmission of the Bible involves meticulous copying and translation efforts, ensuring accessibility globally. In summary, the Bible is a sacred scripture that guides Christian beliefs, practices, and morality, encompassing diverse literary genres and shaping the faith of millions worldwide.

Historical Background

The historical background of the Gospel is set in 1st-century AD Palestine under Roman rule. Second Temple Judaism, with its diverse religious sects, characterized the religious landscape. The cultural milieu blended Jewish traditions, Hellenistic influences, and local customs. Amid messianic expectations, Jesus emerged, teaching about love, compassion, and the Kingdom of God. His crucifixion under Roman authority and subsequent resurrection became central to the Gospel narrative. The historical context enriches the understanding of Jesus' life, teachings, and the development of early Christian communities.

Living the Gospel

Living the Gospel involves embodying Christian teachings found in the New Testament. It means practicing love, compassion, humility, and forgiveness. This includes ethical living, generosity, and responsible stewardship. Central to this lifestyle is a deepened faith, trust in God, and active engagement within the Christian community. It's a continual journey of aligning actions with the transformative principles of Jesus Christ.

FAQ's

The Bible doesn't explicitly address whether pets or animals have souls or if they will be in heaven. However, it establishes that both humans and animals possess the "breath of life," signifying they are living beings (Genesis 2:7, 1:30; 6:17; 7:15, 22). The key distinction is that humans are created in the image of God, implying spiritual, mental, and emotional attributes, and life beyond death. If animals have a soul, it may differ in quality, possibly not persisting after death. Considering animals as part of God's creation in Genesis, with the declaration that they are good (Genesis 1:25), raises the possibility of pets or animals existing in the new earth (Revelation 21:1) or the millennial kingdom (Isaiah 11:6; 65:25). While the Bible doesn't definitively address pets in heaven, trust in God's justice and await His decision on this matter in heaven.

The presence of dinosaurs in the Bible sparks debates within the Christian community on earth's age and Genesis interpretation. Advocates for an older earth believe the Bible doesn't mention dinosaurs, suggesting their extinction millions of years before humans. Those favoring a younger earth argue that the Bible indirectly references dinosaurs using Hebrew words like "tanniyn" and "livyathan." The Bible introduces the behemoth as a remarkable creation, potentially akin to dinosaurs. Global depictions in ancient art and historical accounts, like Marco Polo's, describe encounters with massive serpentine beings in China. The question of dinosaurs in the Bible hinges on one's interpretation. Taking Genesis literally leads to a belief in a young earth with coexisting dinosaurs. While the Bible doesn't explain their fate, proponents suggest environmental changes and human activities led to their extinction post-flood.

The Trinity is the concept of one God existing in three Persons, not implying three separate Gods. While the term "Trinity" is not explicitly in Scripture, the idea is present: 1) One God (Deuteronomy 6:4; 1 Corinthians 8:4; Galatians 3:20; 1 Timothy 2:5). 2) The Trinity consists of three Persons (Genesis 1:1, 26; Matthew 3:16-17; 28:19; 2 Corinthians 13:14). 3) Distinction between the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit is evident (Genesis 19:24; Psalm 2:7; John 14:16-17). 4) Each member is God (John 1:1, 14; Acts 5:3-4; 1 Corinthians 3:16). 5) Subordination exists within the Trinity, an internal relationship (Luke 22:42; John 14:16, 16:13-14). 6) Each member has different tasks (1 Corinthians 8:6; John 1:3; Genesis 1:2). Attempts to illustrate the Trinity fall short; it is a complex, divine mystery. The doctrine has been divisive, but the core aspects are clear in Scripture. Emphasizing God's greatness and His incomprehensible nature is crucial (Romans 11:33-34).
